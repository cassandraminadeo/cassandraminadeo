Cover Letter

Section 1: Introduction & Overview

Technology has shaped my academic and professional path, but understanding how we communicate about technology is what ultimately gives it power. I completed my undergraduate with a degree in computer science and a minor in mathematics at Georgia Southern University. I have always had a love for technology, and the further I got in school, the more I realized that I enjoyed writing about the technology that I was learning about. In the projects I was doing for my classes, I jumped to work on the different types of specification documents that would explain how the code would produce a type of interface or the reasoning behind wanting to do a specific task. My ability to do this gave me the opportunity to receive a job offer and move to Virginia.

During my undergraduate studies, I did a large amount of research and papers in relation to computer security, data communications and networking, software development, and many other technological studies. The complex topics of both computer science and mathematics pushed me to accomplish opportunities to learn how to research, communicate, form ideas, and write those propositions on paper. The highlight of my degree was to articulate the ideas of intricate theories and objectives, and it was something that I thrived in. During this time in school, I knew I wanted to write, but I did not want to give up my love for technology. With a bit of research I was able to determine that technical writing was the career choice for me.

I currently work as a technical writer for a contractor with Lockheed Martin Inc. in Manassas, Va. I started off as the sole technical writer for my program and was responsible for writing technical manuals that will be provided to the Department of Defense. The project I was first involved in supports the International Submarine Program Division, which provides information on the hardware and software that the users will interact with on the ships. Upon completion of this program, I had completed fourteen manuals for the customer. The current project I have the opportunity to work with now involves the use of S1000D and XML to provide Interactive Electronic Technical Manuals (IETMs) to the United States Navy.

When first starting this job, I was very new to writing and the complex information that comes with submarines. I began with doing a great deal of self-training, research, and speaking to the engineers around me on a daily basis. It was a challenging learning curve that I had to accomplish alone, but the work I provided has shown that I am more than capable of the difficulty of this career. I have come to love and appreciate the hard work that comes with the challenges of this kind of writing. In the midst of my career, I began to see the gap of language and understanding rhetoric within the technology field, and having these ideas pushed me to want to pursue my master's in English at Old Dominion University as a remote student.

Throughout my master's degree at ODU, I have been able to build on the knowledge that I had and see how the technical writing field originates while learning its core values and principles. The skills I have learned include the general writing skills I never originally possessed, the basis of rhetoric, and how to communicate the knowledge I have gained in an efficient manner. More importantly, I have been able to relate the theoretical basis of writing to the real-world applications I am engaged with on a day-to-day basis within the industry. As a technical writer working within the programs of the United States Navy, I am able to utilize the rhetorical skills I have gained within my coursework on a regular basis. Theories such as the importance of clear instruction, the use of structured information design, and the importance of ethical communication play a large role in the manner in which I am able to create the technical manuals and interactive media I am engaged with on a regular basis. In a sense, I believe my experience has allowed me to test the theories I have gained within the class, while the class has allowed me to understand the importance of the theories I have gained within the industry.

This portfolio serves the purpose of helping to address the gap that is between technology and writing. The writings I will be providing will help in regard to ensuring how these two fields are absolutely key to each other.

Section 1.1 Course Influences and Gained Knowledge

Over the course of my master’s degree, I have taken nine courses that have shaped my academic experience and inspired the work I am producing today. Each of these classes contributed in different ways, whether by strengthening my research skills, expanding my understanding of rhetoric and technical communication, or challenging me to think more critically about the role of writing in professional spaces.

ENGL 539 Writing in Digital Spaces

Writing in Digital Spaces with Dr. Virginia Tucker was the very first class that I took at Old Dominion University, and it was a great gateway from the world of technology into writing rhetoric. I was already familiar with writing in digital spaces as a technical writer, and this class allowed me to expand on my current knowledge into other forums of technology that I was not as familiar with. In this class one major assignment that I found to be critical in my learning experience was an educational video I created that taught the viewer how to add a contact form to their own personal website. A contact form is a way for a viewer of the website to be able to contact the owner for any questions, comments, or concerns they may have. The video I created shows an easier way to achieve this for new users to avoid using backend coding such as PHP and JavaScript. Throughout this project there was a focus on researchers like Wilbert O. Galitz, who wrote The Essential Guide to User Interface, which assisted in the design elements of designs that we made for this class. This resulted in many features such as callout, video text, and arrows to show and explain how the code is being developed to allow the user to gain a better understanding of what they will be developing. Code is hard, but the video makes it as easy and understanding as possible for all developers to have a different way of learning a difficult concept. Additionally, following the reading of Dànielle Nicole DeVoss, Elyse Eidman-Aadahl, and Troy Hicks, who wrote Because Digital Writing Matters, allowed a focus on the actual writing process that takes place within media spaces. This began the intertwining of technology, academic writing, and teaching rhetoric.

ENGL 715 Professional Writing Theories and Practice

This is the class that introduces the start of research in my academic career. Professional Writing Theories and Practice with Dr. Daniel Richards contained a “Footsteps Paper” assignment where I had to trace every thought, reference, and idea that the authors may have had when doing their research. I did my paper on “Text Recycling in STEM Research: An Exploratory Investigation of Expert and Novice Beliefs and Attitudes” by Cary Moskovitz and Susanne Hall, which is an essay that explores the topic of text recycling (also referred to as self-plagiarism). The topic that I built on correlated with technology, but the process of analyzing their references and theories truly helped build a core foundation for understanding the value of academic research.

Expanding on this knowledge, we interacted with researchers like Carolyn D. Rude in “Mapping the Research Questions in Technical Communication," which follows the idea of text being used to mediate knowledge and our understanding of this field but also discusses the tension that very much still exists between the academic field and the industry. Along with this, we also spent time on Charles Bazerman in his book Shaping Written Knowledge, which allows for the conversation of how rhetoric and learning, specifically with science, exist and thrive.

ENGL 765 Modern Rhetoric/Theory Building & ENGL 760 Classical Rhetoric/Theory Building

Modern and Classical Rhetoric, taught by Dr. Rosaleen Keefe, was a class that I did not expect to enjoy much, but I had a huge gap in knowledge of where rhetoric and the way we wrote even came from. This class developed an appreciation of how rhetoric has built and turned into what it looks like today. I was especially drawn to the mathematical and scientific discussions, particularly the sections from Greek Mathematical Works, specifically Thales to Euclid. Reading the conversations between Cleinias and the Athenian reminded me of the emergence of Platonic realism. In particular, Plato’s dialogue between Socrates and the boy illustrates how mathematical knowledge can be understood as something discovered rather than invented, a concept that strongly shaped my interpretation of these texts.

These classes also introduced me to the side of academic book reviews. In the Modern Rhetoric/Theory Building class, I did a book review on how G. Mitchell Reyes offers a unique perspective of the history of mathematics in his book The Evolution of Mathematics: A Rhetorical Approach. This was the first book I read that was focused on mathematical writing theory and allowed for a better understanding of not only mathematics but also how we teach and convey that crucial information.

ENGL 717 Teaching Professional and Technical Writing

Teaching Professional and Technical Writing, taught by Dr. Daniel Richards, significantly shaped my understanding of how instructional material is developed and delivered in the classroom. While I have experience creating training materials in my professional career, this course deepened my appreciation for the intentional structure, pedagogical strategy, and planning that go into effective instruction. It allowed me to see not just the content itself, but the process behind organizing, sequencing, and presenting that content in a meaningful way. This process can be seen through the work of researchers like Johndan Johnson-Eilola and Stuart A. Selber, who edited Solving Problems in Technical Communication. The book addresses and bridges the gap between theory and practice in technical communication. By the end of the course, I had developed a comprehensive class syllabus and detailed lesson plan centered on teaching students how to create a technical manual, bringing together both my professional experience and newly developed instructional design skills.

ENGL 685 Writing Research Methods

In my final semester, I took Writing Research Methods with Dr. Thea Williamson. This class was a really great demonstration and reflection of what a career in technical writing may actually look like. A portion of our assignments involved speaking with and surveying the people we were conducting research on, which made the experience feel very hands-on and practical. This was an amazing example of how technical writers, both in academia and in the field, are expected to gather information directly from their audience in order to build accurate, thoughtful, and research-based work. Throughout this class, the assistance from the book Qualitative Inquiry and Research Design: Choosing Among Five Approaches by John W. Creswell and Cheryl N. Poth provided a significant amount of insight on how research design is done and how to not only understand how research is created but also how it is understood.

ENGL 706 Visual Rhetoric and Document Design

In Dr. Julia Romberger’s Visual Rhetoric and Document Design class, I placed a strong emphasis on how artificial intelligence is shaping our understanding of both the technological and rhetorical writing fields. AI is making its way into many areas, including book cover design, which became the focus of my project. For this assignment, I analyzed how AI-generated book covers are interpreted by the end user, explored examples of these covers and the audience’s reactions, and examined how AI can be used as a tool rather than a shortcut. Through this work, I found that audiences often respond negatively to book covers created entirely by AI, but I also identified ways AI can be used effectively and ethically. I discovered research showing that Generative Artificial Intelligence (GAI) can enhance book cover design by optimizing elements like color to better appeal to audiences. This project is one I plan to expand on, completing a study replication of "Humans versus AI: Whether and Why We Prefer Human-Created Compared to AI-Created Work," written by Lucas Bellaiche, Rohim Shahi, and Martin Harry Turpin.

There is a direct link between visual images and rhetoric, especially in digital spaces. As researchers, we have an obligation to understand the ethics and professionalism required in this area. Readings such as “Understanding Visual Rhetoric in Digital Writing Spaces” by Mary E. Hocks and “Ethics and Visual Rhetorics: Seeing's Not Believing Anymore” by Nancy Allen helped frame my understanding of how visual rhetoric functions and why ethical responsibility is essential when working with digital and AI-generated content.

ENGL 672 Language Structure Analysis

Language Structure Analysis, taught by Dr. Alla Zareva, was a class that heavily inspired my first empirical study. The book for the class, Longman Student Grammar of Spoken and Written English, written by Susan Conrad, Douglas Biber, Stig Johansson, Edward Finegan, and Geoffrey Leech, was a huge help in understanding and being able to put into practice the analysis that I wanted to do for this class. As a technical writer, I wanted to focus on the fundamental structure of technical documentation, and in this project, I analyzed noun–noun and adjective–noun combinations in computer science journal writings. My goal was to examine how pre-modified nouns contribute to the clarity, precision, and depth of technical writing, highlighting their key role in communicating complex ideas and advancing understanding within the discipline. I plan to expand this assignment to include an analysis of verb–noun combinations, further exploring how these structures support effective technical communication in the field.

ENGL 536 Writing for the Government

Finally, the Writing for the Government class, also taught by Dr. Julia Romberger, was the class that resonated with me the most. As a contractor with the Navy, I was incredibly excited to gain insight into how writing is done within a branch of government. Susan M. Katz is one of the researchers who prompted a more in-depth conversation about how newcomers to a field face a distinct learning curve when learning to write with a specific customer in mind. In “Learning to Write in Organizations," Parts I and II, she examines what newcomers learn about writing on the job and how workplace expectations shape their development as writers.

This class provided valuable context for understanding the rules, expectations, and formalities that exist across different branches of power. The assignment I will be revising for my portfolio will be a newly developed project. I led a team in creating a video game designed to teach users how to avoid phishing scams. Government writing can often be dense and difficult to digest, and this project allowed me to present important information in a more user-friendly and engaging way. In my portfolio, I will explore how educational video games can make complex or technical concepts more accessible, particularly for digital natives who learn best through interactive and entertaining experiences.

Section 2.1 Artificial Intelligence-Created Art - How to Use AI as a Tool

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is becoming increasingly visible across many fields as the technology continues to advance. In my original paper, I examined how AI has been used in book cover art, specifically focusing on A House of Earth and Blood in Sarah J. Maas’s Crescent City series. The cover was later discovered to have been generated using AI, a fact that was not originally disclosed to the public. This raised important questions about transparency and ethics in creative industries.

As I continued my research, I became interested in how the public perceives AI-generated art. Studies show that many people reject AI-made art and tend to view human-created art as more meaningful, emotionally rich, and labor-intensive. These findings suggest that authenticity and human effort still play a significant role in how audiences value creative work. This is the basis of my expansion on this paper by doing a study replication of "Humans versus AI: Whether and Why We Prefer Human-Created Compared to AI-Created Work," written by Lucas Bellaiche, Rohim Shahi, and Martin Harry Turpin, which compares art pieces that are AI-created or human-created labeled.

At the same time, I explored how Generative Artificial Intelligence (GAI) can function as a tool rather than a replacement for human creativity. For example, GAI can help optimize book cover designs to better appeal to target audiences with the use of color harmony and visual composition. While AI is becoming increasingly prevalent in technology and media, it can be used ethically and responsibly when implemented with transparency and clear guidelines.

I chose this topic to examine public perspectives on AI, explore ethical and appropriate uses, and outline best practices for implementation.

Section 2.2 Noun-Noun, Adjective-Noun, & Verb-Noun Combinations in Computer Science Journal Writings

Computer Science is a highly technical and information-dense field that relies heavily on pre-modified nouns to communicate complex ideas clearly and efficiently. In this paper, I analyzed how pre-modified nouns contribute to clarity, precision, and depth in technical writing within the discipline. Specifically, I compared noun–noun and adjective–noun combinations in the Journal of Computer Science abstracts to examine how these lexical structures function in disciplinary writing. My findings highlight the important role these structures play in communicating specialized knowledge and advancing understanding within the field. Understanding how these patterns function can also support the teaching of academic writing, particularly in genre-sensitive language instruction and corpus-based approaches. To extend this research, I plan to analyze verb–noun combinations within the same set of journal abstracts. Verb–noun constructions are commonly used in professional and academic contexts to convey authority and establish a formal tone. Examining these combinations will provide a deeper understanding of how technical documentation is structured and how professionalism is linguistically constructed within academia.

Section 2.3 Phishing for a Good Game: Learning through Interactive Games

This assignment differs from the others because it was not originally written as a paper. Instead, it began as a standalone project. Phishing for a Good Game is a video game that I led my team in creating to demonstrate how complex documentation can be made more accessible, particularly for digital natives. For my portfolio, I am now developing a new writing that uses this project as a UX study while examining how video games more broadly can function as tools for translating complex information into engaging learning experiences. The game is based on the federal document “Phishing Guidance – Stop the Attack Cycle at Phase One," which outlines strategies for preventing phishing attacks. Although the document contains important cybersecurity guidance, it relies heavily on jargon and technical language that may be difficult for younger audiences to fully understand. Our target audience was digital natives—individuals who are highly active online but may not fully recognize the risks of phishing or understand how these attacks operate. In the new portfolio paper, I will analyze how video games as a medium can enhance comprehension, engagement, and retention when teaching complex subjects. Using Phishing for a Good Game as an example, I will explore how interactive design, experiential learning, and gamification can make technical content more accessible. To support this analysis, I plan to compare outcomes between participants who read the original document and those who engage with the game, measuring differences in understanding and retention. This broader examination will help demonstrate how video games can serve as effective educational tools, particularly in fields like cybersecurity where clarity and accessibility are essential.

Together, these projects reflect my commitment to advancing technical communication by integrating academic research with real-world application. Whether examining ethical AI use, exploring game-based learning, or analyzing the linguistic structures of technical documentation, my work centers on improving clarity, accessibility, and effectiveness in complex content. As I transition into a senior technical writer role, I am motivated to apply these insights to support innovation within my organization and contribute to broader conversations in the field. By bridging theory and practice, I aim to strengthen both the discipline of technical writing and the impact it has on the audiences it serves. My academic and professional journey reflects a strong commitment to both technology and communication. With a foundation in computer science and mathematics and experience supporting complex technical documentation for Lockheed Martin, I have consistently worked at the intersection of technical expertise and clear communication. Now, through my MA program at Old Dominion University, I am deepening that connection by studying writing not only as a practical skill but also as an academic discipline that shapes how knowledge is conveyed and understood. As technology continues to evolve rapidly, the ability to communicate its complexities clearly and responsibly becomes increasingly essential and something that I hope to convey in this portfolio.
